Egypt Has a Sexual Harassment Problem

ByHazem Moghni November 8, 2024November 9, 2024

Sexual harassment is rampant in Egypt — to the point that it repels tourists from visiting one of the world’s oldest civilizations.

Excuse me for missing two Friday Blog posts before this one; a bit has happened — I got a job and college applications started getting real. Thank goodness I delivered on this one, since Trump winning the U.S. presidential election would have been a good enough excuse not to! It’s very weird, but every country to its own, I guess.

How Big?

Your first question is probably, “Really, how big of a problem is sexual harassment in Egypt?” The answer is: very.

The UNFPA once described Egypt as “second in the world after Afghanistan in terms of this issue [sexual harassment].”1 While Egypt hasn’t conducted a survey to measure this issue for over a decade, it is likely that we are no longer second globally (hooray!). Still, according to the latest two surveys done in 20082 (by the Egyptian Center for Women’s Rights) and 20133 (by UN Women), the majority of Egyptian women report experiencing sexual harassment, with percentages of 83% and 99%, respectively.

Whenever I share these statistics with my male friends, they are always super surprised and skeptical. They typically ask, in this order: How big is the sample? How did they define “harassment”? And wouldn’t these women’s rights organizations want a larger percentage to showcase that the problem is big (perhaps even bigger than it actually is)?

The answers to these questions are as follows: 1,010 women were surveyed in 2008, and over 2,330 in 2013. The definition of harassment included physical touching, not just verbal harassment; in fact, in the 2013 study, 91.5% of respondents reported that “part of their bodies were touched.” As for the final question, of course these organizations would benefit from finding a larger percentage — after all, “99% of Egyptian women are being harassed” is quite the title. While it is unlikely that UN Women would falsify data, we cannot ignore the potential bias in a report, even if it’s a report with a noble purpose.

So, does that mean the problem is not real? Of course not.

More Evidence

The two organizations may be biased, the sample sizes may be small, and the studies may be outdated, but the fact remains that, even without up-to-date statistics, sexual harassment is real here. Just ask any woman you know, or better yet, try this simple solution:

Go to www.reddit.com/search, put in “Egypt,” sort by Top, and view the comments. Excluding the “fun” facts about Ancient Egypt, you will find people saying variations of “Don’t visit Egypt if you are a woman.” Also, there are many videos and posts from foreign women describing their awful experiences when visiting. In addition, when the Egyptian Center for Women’s Rights stated that “83% of Egyptian women have been harassed,” they also noted that 98% of foreign women have experienced harassment.

Still not convinced? Consider this: the first jail sentence in Egypt for sexual harassment was handed down in… 20084. To put things in perspective, everyone born before 1990 — 50 million people, or nearly half the population — grew up without even having the concept that jail sentences could be given for sexual harassment. Wild, isn’t it?

The problem is real.

Just a Few Bad Apples… Right?

Not quite. According to the 2008 study, 62% of the 1,011 interviewed men admitted (emphasis on admitted!) that they had harassed a woman before. These men were from Cairo (Shubra Masr and Ain Shams), Giza (Imbaba and Dokki), and Qalyubia (Shubra and Banha), which is not very diverse. The UN study, while it didn’t ask men if they harassed women, had a more diverse distribution across seven governorates, rather than just three. Places like Shubra Masr and Imbaba are low socioeconomic areas, so the percentage of admitted harassment may be inflated compared to the (unknown) real national percentage.

With such a small and not very diverse sample distribution, you might lean toward thinking that it’s just a few bad apples. But it’s important to realize that this percentage reflects only those who admitted to harassment. Given the heinous nature of the act, self-reporting is likely to be lower than the actual number.

Even if it is ONLY these places (which is unlikely) that have a majority of harassers, you have to recognize that, at the very least, Egypt has areas where most men admit to having sexually harassed a woman — and that women live in those places! For context, the surveyed areas are very populated; Greater Cairo has over 20 million people.

What Is the Solution?

The first step is to shift the taboo from the victim to the aggressor, to stigmatize the act of harassment and stop victim-blaming. In a 2008 study, 54% of men blamed sexual harassment of women on the women themselves! In fact, I got the idea to write this blog after seeing people blame the victim on the h*llhole that is Twitter (follow me over there, by the way; I chronically tweet every day!).

Here is a crazy take (crazy in Egypt, only!): a woman’s choice of clothing can’t be at fault for her harassment. It can be a cause (i.e., increase the likelihood of harassment), but it is not her fault—at all. You must distinguish between something being a cause and something being a fault; the only fault is the aggressor’s. Here’s an overly simple analogy (just to help you make the distinction): imagine you leave your house unlocked, and your house gets robbed. Sure, you did something that increased the likelihood of that happening to you, but you are not at fault (the robber is), and thinking otherwise would be absurd! I said “overly simple analogy” because, unlike locking your home, wearing modestly doesn’t ward off harassment; Egyptian women are among the most modestly dressed in the world, and yet Egypt makes the leaderboard of creep Olympiads.

We cannot fix a problem we refuse to believe is real, so acknowledge it first!

Then move on to the second step. Starting with yourself, never harass physically or verbally — through ogling, catcalling, stalking, touching, or “joking.” Lead by example; it’s obvious but necessary. The third step is to spread that mentality: educate those around you, shut down objectifying conversations, oppose victim-blamers, and prevent harassment. Stop the bad you see! The fourth and final step is to keep going; if you live in Egypt, every day might feel like hope is slipping away, but remember that change takes time. Give it time, and who knows, maybe the next survey will find that most men aren’t offenders.

Closing

I am still hesitant about publishing this post. Hesitant because I know the reaction won’t be entirely positive, hesitant because the concept of blaming the criminal is too “woke” here, hesitant because I know I will face disapproval and have to reconsider some friendships, hesitant because the problem is real.

But it shouldn’t be, so I click Publish.
