Introduction and Warning

Inspired by Kan Harris’ guide, I wrote easy instructions I can send to my close contacts to up their privacy/security. But anyone can (and should!) use this. Enjoy!

First, it’s important to point out that maximum cyber security/privacy doesn’t exist – at least not for us normal citizens (Yes, I’m a title-baiter). Security holes are always found. Data breaches often happen. You’ll occasionally mess up. You get the idea.

If the CIA wanted to know everything about you, they will – unless you dedicated a large portion of your life to prevent that from happening. We will not be learning how to do that. If you want to hide from overpowered intelligence agencies, this is not the guide for you.

But for what it is worth, most countries’ intelligence agencies (including Egypt’s) aren’t sophisticated as the CIA or Mossad. And most importantly, you are most likely not individually targeted by any intelligence agency… hopefully…. right?

Anyways, we can achieve a high level of security and privacy while maintaining convenience (impossible if you want to maximize everything and truly be untraceable). This is what I’ll be focusing on. I will also include some advanced tips if that interests you.

As far as I know, any important piece of software here is safe and free-of-charge (except for Windows 11 Pro).

WARNING: You’re liable for everything you do to your devices – I’m merely but an adviser. Please don’t sue me if you break your devices/programs.

Prerequisites

Some definitions you need to understand:

  1. Privacy: Control over personal information; freedom from intrusion.
    • For example, using the restroom is private but not anonymous.
  2. Security: Protection from external threats.
    • For example, if you’re unhackable, you’re secure.
  3. Anonymity: Having an identity that is unknown.
    • For example, a masked superhero is anonymous but not private.

My writing heavily focuses on privacy and security. For absolute anonymity, see the (advanced) Hitchhiker’s Guide to Online Anonymity.

Optimizing Windows & Increasing Privacy

First, make sure your OS is up-to-date. Restart if needed.

Debloating & Removing Telemetry

Manually

We’ll do some stuff manually then use a tool.

Go to Startup Apps (Windows Search) and stop anything that doesn’t need to start right away. Generally, over 90% of programs should be set to off.

Go to System Configuration (Windows Search) then Boot then Advanced Options and set the number of processors to the max.

In System Configuration again: go to Services, click Hide all Microsoft services then click disable all. (Don’t worry; if a service is needed it’ll start!)

For advanced users: you can go to Services (Windows Search) and disable start-up services. If you don’t know what a service does, know before you break anything.

Now go to Task Scheduler (Windows Search) and disable unnecessary programs like Edge and OneDrive starting at whatever random times they’re set to run.

Finally, follow this guide to disable the AWFUL Taskbar Edge Search that’s auto-activated when you mistype while trying to Windows Search your files.

Tool

Now onto the easy stuff. Go to christitus.com/windows-tool to copy the command into a Run-as-Admin PowerShell… or just copy this:

iwr -useb https://christitus.com/win | iex

Press enter to run the command. Go to Tweaks. Select:

  1. Delete Temporary Files
  2. Disable ConsumerFeatures
  3. Disable Telemetry
  4. Disable Location Tracking (unless you use it)
  5. Disable Storage Sense
  6. Disable Wifi-Sense
  7. Run Disk Cleanup
  8. Set Services to Manual
  9. Debloat Edge
  10. The following if you have Adobe products
    • Adobe Network Block (unless you need/use it)
    • Adobe Debloat
  11. Disable Background Apps
  12. Disable Microsoft Copilot
  13. Remove Microsoft Edge (if you want [unlikely to work])
  14. Remove OneDrive (if you want)

Then in “Customize Preferences” turn on:

  • Show File Extensions

And customize your preferences (in “Customize Preferences” ONLY) if you’d like.

Click Run Tweaks.

For advanced users: you can go to https://www.w10privacy.de/, download W10Privacy, and configure stuff further to limit data-gathering. You gotta know what you’re doing, though. Read, understand, and Google (as in use a search engine not use Google) every option if you really want to configure further using W10Privacy.

Security

Router

Not computer-related. For your router,

  1. Set strong WiFi passwords for both your network and admin page.
    • Change the netwrok’s password periodically. Every one year is fine.
  2. Enable the highest WiFi encryption mode: WPA3 (highest), WPA2 (high), or WPA (don’t).
  3. Have at least two SSIDs: one for home users and one for guests.
  4. Change the two (or more) network names from their default names.
  5. Turn off UPnP.
  6. Turn off WPS.
  7. Do not hide your SSID(s). It’s not actually “hidden” from anyone who can hack a network.
    • Hackers can see your “hidden” network using tools. (You can too BTW!)
    • In ADDITION, “hidden” is ALSO less private and secure. Your devices would be broadcasting signalS trying to find it everywhere (not just near your home!). When you’re outside, hackers can exploit that to identify you and/or your home location. They can even trick your device into connecting to a malicious network. Don’t hide your SSID.
  8. Optional: you could turn on DNS filtering here (rather than the in OS-level, as you’ll see down below).
    • DNS filtering is using the Domain Name System to block connections to websites because they’re malicious, privacy-invasive, annoying, inappropriate, or whatever reason you choose (yes, you can manually block certain websites here.)
    • For DNS filtering, Quad9 and DNS.eu are recommended (use only one of them).

Hardening Windows

Windows Antivirus

For Windows, its own up-to-date antivirus (AV) is the best free one. Don’t use free AVs; they’re either worse or as good but slower.

To harden Windows’ AV, download ConfigureDefender. Then, run it and choose High. Done! Wasn’t that easy? The Max setting is not recommended for a normal user because it’ll break some stuff. If you’re an advanced user, go ahead!

You can, of course, go through Windows Security and edit. In App & browser control you’ll find Smart App Control, which is good. Turn it on.

WARNING: If Smart App Control ever got turned off (even during Windows installation), you gotta reinstall Windows to activate it.

Firewall

Remember when I said Windows AV is good? I lied. It’s mostly good; its firewall is bad. Download a third party firewall that’s FOSS (Free and open-source software) and reputable. I recommend Portmaster. In it, you can

  1. turn on DNS Filtering. Choose Quad9.
  2. add filter lists. Choose
    • Analytics and Telemetry in “Ads & Trackers”
    • Malware
    • Deception
    • Mixed Ads/Trackers/Malware
    • Unbreak Popular Websites
    • Whatever else you like. You could even block Microsoft as a whole to prevent telemetry by 100%, but it’d break some sites like LinkedIn and GitHub.
  3. block programs. Like Microsoft Edge.
  4. block domains.
  5. review all connections, and, when experiencing broken sites, unblock some domains to fix the issue, which is a bit inconvenient, but it’s for your own good.
    • Make sure you don’t unblock an unsafe website.
 BitLocker

You can (and should!) use BitLocker to encrypt your disk so that your files are private in case your devices/disk is stolen or lost. If you choose to do this, choose AES-256 encryption.

See BitLocker guides online. It’s simple. Just make sure to have the passcode somewhere safe in your house.

Online Security

Cloud Encryption

You can also use Cryptomater to encrypt files before uploading them to any cloud service.

Why? A lot of cloud services invasively scan/read your files, including Google Drive and OneDrive. With Cryptomater, all they’ll be able to scan are incomprehensible encrypted files – in your face, Big Tech companies! If you do choose to use Cryptomater, you can use whatever cloud service. OneDrive would be a good pick, considering it’ll work well with windows.

OPTIONAL: If you’re still feeling uneasy about using privacy-invasive cloud services (you shouldn’t if your files are encrypted), you could self-host a cloud using services like Nextcloud.

Extensions

No need for an adblocker since you got Brave’s built-in shield.

FYI: uBlock Origin (uBO) is the best adblocker. But beware: you’ll get bad results if you turn on two adblockers at the same time (in this case: Brave Shield and uBO). For Firefox and its forks, use uBO without any additions.

You will need a password manager. Bitwarden is among the best (if not the best) cloud-based password manager.

You might think: “Storing ALL my passwords on the cloud; isn’t that anti-private?!” Not exactly. Bitwarden is encrypted. “Everything is encrypted?” you might ask. The answer is no. Your email address and the dates you created/password are not encrypted. Other than that, you’re good.

For advanced users: Still anxious? Use KeePass to store passwords locally on your device or better yet write them on paper stored in your house. But then you risk losing you password; you device/disk can be destroyed/stolen, and papers are often lost/burned. You choose: maximum security and inconvenience or high security and convenience.

Back to Bitwarden. You need an email to create an account, which brings us to the next section.

Cyber Hygiene

Email

ProtonMail.

Secure. End-to-end encrypted (E2EE). Based in Switzerland (strict privacy laws). Open source. Blah blah blah. In short: it’s the best. A close second is Tuta.

In ProtonMail can even create aliases to sign up to sites using different email addresses that receive and forward mails to your true email address, as to not expose/share it. If you do choose to use Bitwarden, I advise you dedicate an email address to it and sign up using an alias.

WARNING: Some websites don’t like aliases. I think GitHub is among them. So if you’re changing your email for some accounts you care about, try to check if the site you’re using bans/flags accounts that use aliases.

Passwords

Use randomly generated passwords that include letters, numbers, and special characters. 12+ characters is good. Increasing passwords’ length won’t hurt, especially since you’ll be saving them to your secure and private password manager (like Bitwarden).

WARNING: Don’t use your browser’s default password manager or your built-in phone password manager.

Use a very, very hard mastercode (that unlocks the password manager). Memorize it and write it on a piece of paper hidden somewhere safe.

Two-factor Authentication (2FA)

You need two-factor authentication to secure your accounts.

SMS authentication is a big no-no. Go turn it off on all your accounts RIGHT NOW! Okay maybe not now now, but turn it off because SMS messages are not E2EE and they can be intercepted. The most secure option is a physical key like YubiKey if you got money.

But this is a free guide and 2FA apps are more than enough for 99% of people. The best 2FA apps are Aegis (for Android) and Ente Auth (for iOS). Both are private and secure, but Aegis is better as far as I know. For added privacy, add the sites manually to your 2FA app rather than scanning QR codes.

More on smartphones below.

Common Sense

This might be the most important piece of advice here; on the internet, you need to have common sense and be skeptical. There’s a saying that goes, “The best antivirus is between you and the keyboard (i.e., common sense),” or something like that. When online, you gotta flip your prespective: everyone and everything is guilty (i.e., privacy-invasive and/or malicious) until proven innocent. It’s honestly saddening how malicious the internet is…

Anyways! Here are some more tips:

  1. Don’t answer security questions (e.g., place of birth) in “fun” online forms.
  2. Don’t share your answers to those online forms on your public social media accounts.
  3. Don’t share your passwords with anybody.
  4. Don’t run executables (files ending with .exe) from any site. Only download from the official source site.
  5. Don’t register for weird/new/small sites using the same recovery email you use for your accounts.
    • With ProtonMail, you can dedicate an email address for spam, and register using 10 different aliases to register for such sites.
  6. Don’t re-use any passwords.
  7. Listen to your hardened Windows Antivirus.
  8. Ask yourself, “Is it possible that the file that my antivirus blocked is unsafe?” Investigate and answer.
    • And remember, there’s no stronger antivirus than YOU.
  9. Don’t overshare on public posts/forums.
    • More on deleting past digital footprint below.
  10. Go through the privacy/security settings and configure every service you start using.
  11. OPTIONAL: Read the privacy policies before singing up for anything (or at least most things).
    • Easy alternative: Use tosdr.org for a summary and/or see their reputation on online forums like Reddit. r/privacy is a good place to see.
  12. You get the idea.

Cleaning Digital Footprint

Now that you have security, privacy, and good cyber hygiene practices, it’s time to clean past errors.

After reseting your iPhone (if you do choose to do that), make sure you disallow Apple gathering analytics.

  • Messaging
    • Yes, this is also part of your digital footprint. Not public. But it’s there, and more often than not, it has the most private information about you. Not everyone needs a record of every message you sent.
    • Permanent messages: You can’t unsend messages on WhatsApp (after 48 hours), email, Twitter (X), or TikTok. Keep that in mind from now on!
    • Disposable messages: You can unsend messages on Messenger, Telegram, Discord, and Instagram.
    • Auto-deletion: Set an auto-delete timer (one week) for WhatsApp, Telegram, Signal, Briar, etc.. This is very useful in case your phone gets stolen. If there are messages that are dear to you, you can select and keep them.
      • On the topic of Telegram, if you do choose to use, know it’s not E2EE by default. To activate that you need to “start a secret conversation” with someone, which is a feature that only works for DMs. Telegram is more of a social media app than a private messaging app.
  • Social Media
    • You can request to see your data from Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, Discord and even Google and Apple. See the data. Determine how much you want/can delete.You can delete all of your history on Facebook and Instagra
      • WARNING: Reddit is “anonymous,” but your identity can be figured out rather easily based on your public activity.
  • Old Accounts
    • a: Go to all your password manager(s); connected accounts in the settings, browser, Google, Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter; and search the following in all your emails: sign up, sign in, verify, and password.
    • b: Google your full name(s) with and without quotations.
    • c: Google your username(s) with and without quotations.
    • After doing a, b, and c you now have all your accounts. If you don’t remember a password reset using your email(s)/phone(s).
    • Log in to all accounts, private everything that can be privated, and turn off any data-collection. Then replace your info (name, email, phone, picture, etc.) with gibberish.
    • Then delete your account. Use justdeleteme to see how to delete your account. Also, from now on, use that that website before creating any acount!
    • Sometimes you gotta email them to get your account deleted.
    • Other times you can’t delete your accounts; in that case replace your login info (email, phone, password, etc.) with gibberish and log out. Use Temp Mail to put temporary disposable emails. For phone, search the web until you find a service that works for that website you’re trying to fill with gibberish info.
  • Emails & Numbers
    • Google all your emails and phone numbers with and without the “+2” (because it be breached in two different formats).
    • Use Have I Been Pwned and Epieos.
      • Delete/stop using email addresses or phone numbers that have been breached. Try to private any info you find on Epieos.
    • Login to all your email addresses and accounts (on all platforms) and turn off SMS authentication. Replace it with 2FA. Disconnect unnecessary platforms.
  • Further OSINTing (Open-source intelligence)
    • Steps aren’t as clear cut here.
    • See an OSINT map like this one, read guides, and watch videos to learn how to stalk (yourself) using the public web. You can also ask your friends to stalk you and tell you what they found.
    • See how much you can gather about yourself starting from different pieces of info like phone number, username, full name, profile picture, email, etc.
    • All that you find publicly is findable by anybody.
    • Omit any private info you find and don’t like.
      • How? By logging in and deleting/privating an account/post OR contacting the site to remove/private your data, etc.
    • Do these steps periodically. Every year is fine.
